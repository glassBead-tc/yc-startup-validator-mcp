# Topic Literature Review Orchestration

This workflow synthesizes academic research on a given scientific or technical topic by searching research papers and combining with foundational knowledge from Wikipedia to create comprehensive literature reviews.

## Overview

Creating a thorough literature review requires systematically discovering, analyzing, and synthesizing academic research while providing proper context and identifying research gaps. This orchestration combines academic paper searches with encyclopedic background to produce well-structured literature reviews that highlight key findings, debates, methodological approaches, and future research directions.

## Required Tools

- `research_paper_search_exa` - Academic paper discovery
- `wikipedia_search_exa` - Background and context
- `web_search_exa` - Additional academic sources

## Best Practices

1. **Systematic Approach**: Follow structured methodology for reproducibility
2. **Multiple Databases**: Search across various academic sources
3. **Quality Over Quantity**: Focus on high-impact, peer-reviewed work
4. **Balanced Perspective**: Include diverse viewpoints and methodologies
5. **Clear Documentation**: Maintain transparent search and selection criteria

## Advanced Techniques

### Citation Network Analysis
- Map influential papers and authors
- Identify research clusters
- Track idea evolution
- Find seminal works
- Discover emerging trends

### Meta-Analytic Integration
- Combine effect sizes statistically
- Assess heterogeneity
- Identify moderators
- Calculate overall effects
- Evaluate publication bias

### Systematic Review Methods
- PRISMA guidelines application
- Risk of bias assessment
- Quality scoring systems
- Evidence grading
- Sensitivity analysis

### Bibliometric Analysis
- Publication trends over time
- Geographic distribution
- Collaboration networks
- Journal impact patterns
- Funding source analysis

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Cherry-Picking**: Include contradictory findings
2. **Recency Bias**: Don't ignore foundational older work
3. **Language Bias**: Acknowledge non-English literature
4. **Publication Bias**: Consider unpublished works
5. **Over-Synthesis**: Maintain nuance in conclusions
