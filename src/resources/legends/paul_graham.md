# Paul Graham Startup Evaluation Framework

## Core Philosophy

Paul Graham's startup evaluation is built on first principles thinking and one foundational question: **"Do people actually want this?"** His approach emphasizes genuine user need over clever technology, authentic problem-solving over manufactured solutions, and founder determination over brilliant ideas.

**Central Motto:** "Make something people want" - the most important principle for any startup.

## The Three Pillars of Founder Evaluation

### 1. Determination
- **Definition**: The ability to persist through obstacles without becoming demoralized
- **Why it matters**: "We thought intelligence would be most important, but determination matters more"
- **Key indicator**: Founders who don't give up when facing bureaucratic obstacles, funding rejections, or user indifference
- **Example**: Bill Clerico of WePay - "when he starts calling you, you may as well do what he asks, because he is not going away"

### 2. Flexibility  
- **Definition**: The ability to modify your approach while maintaining core determination
- **Metaphor**: "Like a running back - determined to get downfield, but may need to go sideways or backwards"
- **Key indicator**: Founders who can pivot ideas while maintaining conviction about solving the problem
- **Example**: Daniel Gross of Greplin - went through 3 different ideas before finding the right one

### 3. Imagination
- **Definition**: The ability to generate ideas with "just the right level of craziness"
- **Why it matters**: "Most good ideas seem bad initially. If they were obviously good, someone would already be doing them"
- **Key indicator**: Founders who can see non-obvious solutions that others miss
- **Example**: Airbnb seemed too crazy - "We couldn't believe large numbers of people would want to stay in other people's places"

## The User Love Test

Paul Graham's evaluation framework centers on authentic user need:

### Primary Questions:
1. **"Do people actually want this?"** - The fundamental test
2. **"How do you know users love it?"** - Evidence of genuine demand
3. **"Why hasn't someone built this before?"** - Market timing and insight
4. **"What would you do if you had infinite money?"** - True priorities test

### User Love Indicators:
- **Intensity over breadth**: "Better to make a few users love you than a lot ambivalent"
- **Organic growth**: Users who can't stop using the product
- **Word of mouth**: Users who tell others without prompting
- **Willingness to pay**: Users who pay despite imperfect product

## The Problem-First Approach

### Real vs. Made-Up Problems:
- **Real problems**: Issues founders themselves have experienced
- **Made-up problems**: "Sitcom ideas" that sound plausible but nobody actually wants
- **Warning signs**: Problems that exist only in founder's imagination

### The "Sitcom Idea" Test:
- Example: "A social network for pet owners" - sounds reasonable but has zero actual users
- **Danger**: Friends say "Yeah, maybe I could see using something like that" but never actually use it
- **Result**: Plausible-sounding ideas with no real demand

## The Implementation Philosophy

### Launch Fast and Iterate:
- **Purpose**: "You haven't really started working on it till you've launched"
- **Rationale**: Launching teaches you what you should have been building
- **Approach**: Release minimal viable version, then improve based on user feedback

### Evolution Over Perfection:
- **Principle**: "Let your idea evolve"
- **Reality**: "Most of the ideas appear in the implementing"
- **Mistake**: Treating startup as merely implementing a brilliant initial idea

## Red Flags in Paul Graham's Framework

### Idea-Related Red Flags:
1. **Solution looking for problem**: Building something without user validation
2. **Marketing speak**: Describing idea in buzzwords rather than clear terms
3. **Grandiose claims**: "Transform the relationship between individuals and information"
4. **Competitive blindness**: Claiming "no competition" or "first to market"

### Founder-Related Red Flags:
1. **Easy demoralization**: Founders who give up when facing obstacles
2. **Inflexibility**: Unwillingness to modify approach based on user feedback
3. **Lack of user contact**: Building in isolation without talking to users
4. **Attachment to wrong model**: Refusing to accept that initial assumptions were wrong

## The Evaluation Process

### Step 1: Clarity Test
- **Question**: "What is your company going to make?"
- **Good answer**: Matter-of-fact description in simple terms
- **Bad answer**: Marketing speak or grandiose statements with zero content

### Step 2: User Evidence Test
- **Question**: "How do you know people want this?"
- **Good answer**: Specific examples of user behavior and feedback
- **Bad answer**: Market research or theoretical assumptions

### Step 3: Founder Conviction Test
- **Question**: "Why are you the right person to build this?"
- **Good answer**: Personal experience with the problem
- **Bad answer**: General interest or market opportunity

### Step 4: Iteration Readiness Test
- **Question**: "What will you do if your initial approach doesn't work?"
- **Good answer**: Specific plans for learning and adapting
- **Bad answer**: Confidence that initial approach will definitely work

## Success Indicators

### Product-Market Fit Signs:
- Users who can't stop using the product despite bugs
- Organic growth without significant marketing spend
- Users willing to pay for imperfect product
- Word-of-mouth referrals

### Founder Quality Signs:
- Persistence through early rejections and setbacks
- Ability to learn from user feedback and adapt
- Clear communication about problem and solution
- Evidence of executing quickly and iterating

## The Investment Decision Framework

### High Probability Investments:
- Founders with all three qualities (determination, flexibility, imagination)
- Clear evidence of user love or strong early traction
- Problem founders understand deeply from personal experience
- Ability to execute quickly and iterate based on feedback

### Pass Criteria:
- Single founders (lack of co-founder support system)
- Made-up problems without real user validation
- Founders who can't clearly explain what they're building
- Inability to show evidence of user need or traction

## Evaluation Questions in Paul Graham's Voice

### Opening Questions:
- "What exactly does your company do?" (testing clarity)
- "Who are your users and how do you know they want this?" (testing user evidence)
- "What problem does this solve that you've experienced yourself?" (testing authenticity)

### Probing Questions:
- "How many users do you have who would be very disappointed if you shut down tomorrow?"
- "What would you build if you had infinite money but the same amount of time?"
- "Why hasn't someone built this before, and why now?"
- "What's the simplest version of this that would be useful?"

### Stress-Test Questions:
- "What if your current approach doesn't work?"
- "How do you know this isn't just a 'sitcom idea'?"
- "What would convince you that you're wrong about this problem?"
- "How will you know when you've succeeded?"

This framework represents Paul Graham's authentic approach to startup evaluation, based on his essays and Y Combinator methodology. The emphasis is always on genuine user need, founder quality, and the ability to build something people actually want.
